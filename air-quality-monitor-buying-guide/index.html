How to choose the right air quality monitor

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose an air quality monitor based on the pollutants you need to track (PM2.5, VOCs, CO2), prioritize laser-based sensors for accuracy, and decide whether a standalone display or a companion app fits your habits. Well-reviewed models from brands like Airthings, PurpleAir, and Temtop offer reliable readings without breaking the bank.

What should I look for in an air quality monitor?

Start with the sensor type. The most important differentiator is whether the monitor uses a laser particle counter or an infrared sensor for particulate matter. Laser sensors are far more accurate at detecting fine particles like smoke and dust, while infrared sensors are cheaper but will miss smaller particles. Reviewers consistently recommend laser-based models for anyone serious about tracking air quality. Next, consider the list of pollutants it measures. A basic monitor might only track PM2.5 and temperature/humidity. A more complete unit adds CO2 (a proxy for ventilation) and total volatile organic compounds (TVOCs) from paints, cleaners, and cooking. Many users find CO2 readings especially useful for knowing when to open a window. Finally, think about how you’ll get the data. Some monitors have a bright display you can glance at; others require an app. If you want real-time alerts or historical trends, app-connected models are essential. Owner feedback shows that a clear, simple display wins for at-a-glance use, while app-focused units often have better logging and sharing features.

Which pollutants should my monitor measure?

The most commonly measured and actionable pollutant is PM2.5. fine particles that penetrate deep into the lungs. If you live in an area with wildfire smoke, heavy traffic, or use a wood stove, a monitor with a good PM2.5 sensor is non-negotiable. Most consumer monitors use laser scattering to count particles, and the best ones can detect particles down to very small sizes. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is another key metric. Elevated CO2 levels indicate poor ventilation, which can make you feel drowsy or give you headaches. Monitors with a CO2 sensor (often using nondispersive infrared technology) are particularly helpful in bedrooms, home offices, or classrooms. Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) come from cleaning products, new furniture, paint, and cooking. A TVOC sensor can alert you to unseen chemical buildup. However, these sensors are less specific, they can’t tell you exactly which chemical is present. Still, for everyday awareness, a combination of PM2.5, CO2, and TVOC covers the most common indoor concerns.

How accurate are consumer air quality monitors?

Consumer-grade monitors are not as precise as the professional instruments used by environmental agencies, but they are accurate enough to be useful for day-to-day decisions. Laser-based PM2.5 sensors typically agree within 10 to 20 percent of reference-grade devices, according to published comparisons. That margin is fine for knowing whether the air is good, moderate, or hazardous. Infrared CO2 sensors found in most consumer units are also reasonably accurate, usually within 30–50 ppm of lab-grade sensors. Calibration drift over time is the main concern; some monitors auto-calibrate in fresh air, while others need manual baseline resets. Owner reviews suggest that models with self-calibration hold up better over months of use. For VOCs, accuracy is more variable. TVOC sensors give a general trend rather than an absolute number. They are excellent at detecting changes (like a fresh coat of paint) but less reliable for comparing one room to another. If absolute precision matters, you’ll need a much more expensive instrument, but for typical home use, a well-reviewed consumer monitor is plenty.

Should I get a monitor with a screen or an app only?

A monitor with a built-in screen gives you instant visual feedback without pulling out your phone. Models with a color-coded AQI display or numerical PM2.5 readout are popular for countertops and bedside tables. Many owners say this “at a glance” convenience makes them more aware of short-lived spikes from cooking or cleaning. App-only monitors, or those that rely on a phone for full data, are often smaller and cheaper. They can still alert you with push notifications, and the app usually provides detailed charts and exportable data. The trade-off is that you must have your phone handy, and if the app isn’t well maintained, the monitor can feel like a dumb brick. Many of the best-reviewed monitors offer both a screen and an app. That combination gives you the immediacy of a display and the depth of historical analysis. If you’re choosing between the two, think about where you’ll place it: a living room or kitchen benefits from a screen; a remote sensor in a spare room can get away with app-only.

How much should I spend on a quality air quality monitor?

You can find basic PM2.5-and-temperature monitors at a very budget-friendly price point. These often use infrared sensors and limited displays; they’ll give you a rough idea of particle levels but may miss fine particles and lack durability. Owner feedback on the cheapest models frequently mentions inconsistent readings and short battery life. Step up to the mid-priced range, roughly the cost of a good pair of sneakers, and you get laser-based PM2.5 sensors, CO2 measurement, and often a companion app. This is the sweet spot for most households. Brands like Airthings and Temtop are consistently praised here for reliability and sensor accuracy. Premium monitors, typically costing several times more, add things like multiple sensors (formaldehyde, radon), larger color displays, and integration with smart-home systems. If you have specific health concerns or want the most thorough data, these can be worth the investment. But for the vast majority of users, a thoughtful mid-priced model will tell you everything you need to keep your indoor air healthy.

Frequently asked questions

Do I really need an air quality monitor at home?

Not everyone does, but it’s invaluable if you experience unexplained allergies, live in an area prone to wildfires or heavy traffic, or spend a lot of time indoors. Even a basic PM2.5 monitor can show you when the air is getting bad so you can close windows or run a purifier.

Can an air quality monitor detect mold?

No consumer monitor directly detects mold spores. However, a sudden spike in PM levels or a change in humidity readings (if your monitor includes a humidity sensor) can alert you to conditions where mold may grow. Persistent high humidity combined with elevated particles is worth investigating further.

What is a good AQI reading for indoor air?

For indoor spaces, a PM2.5 reading under 12 µg/m³ (or an AQI under 50) is considered good. Many monitors use a color scale: green means clean, yellow is moderate, orange and red indicate unhealthy. Keep in mind that indoor air should ideally be even cleaner than outdoor standards, especially for sensitive individuals.

Do I need to calibrate my air quality monitor?

Most consumer monitors come precalibrated and require no user adjustment for years. CO2 sensors may drift slightly over time; models with an automatic baseline calibration (ABC) compensate by assuming low outdoor CO2 levels during ventilation. If your monitor lacks ABC, occasional manual calibration by placing it in fresh air may be recommended by the manufacturer.

Is a laser particle counter better than an infrared one?

Yes, laser-based counters are significantly more accurate for PM2.5 because they can detect smaller particles and distinguish them better. Infrared sensors are cheaper but often miss fine smoke and cannot give reliable particle counts below a few microns. For health-relevant measurements, a laser sensor is strongly preferable.

In shortAn air quality monitor is a practical tool for taking control of your indoor environment. Focus on the sensors that match your biggest concerns, choose a model with a laser particle counter for reliable PM2.5 data, and don’t overspend on features you won’t use. A solid mid-range monitor with a clear display and companion app will serve most households well for years.
